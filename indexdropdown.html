I made this track in Berlin in 2013 after reading the synopsis to the movie Limitless. About a man who uses nootropic "smart drugs" to achieve economic and political success in the world, the movie seemed to have potential in exploring themes such as whether brain chemistry determined one's fate in life, how the pharmaceutical industry would shape society in the future in giving tools to alter it, and how success itself is defined within society in the first place. I didn't end up watching the movie but I was inspired by those themes and made a short, imaginary score for it. Taking those ideas as a starting point, however, I couldn't really help but wonder how a psychoneurological disposition towards achieving truly "limitless" success would function in a society with an economic model based on unregulated economic competition, and the incentives that motivate the pharmaceutical industry in the real world. The track consequently ended up belonging to a genre I can only describe as "dystopian industrialism," music for some cartoonishly banal alternate universe in which individuals languish in jail for low-level drug offenses while corporations prosper from mass opiate distribution, hedge fund managers monopolize the market for life-saving drugs in order to demand exorbitant prices, and grandiose billionaires achieve political success on a platform of destroying public health. Named for a psychological classification called the "dark triad" consisting of three traits — narcissism, Machiavellianism, and psychopathy — the track was inspired by the question of whether that which is defined as a psychological disorder on the individual scale might be incentivized on the collective scale. It's worth noting here that criminal drug enterprise, the accompanying mentality, and its effect on the community has acquired folk status as a musical subject — particularly in rap music, where the genre is a multi-billion dollar industry unto itself — so the concept or spirit of this piece was partially to imagine and thematically explore that phenomenon through an inverted lense, from the perspective of society's dominant power structures.

I have been making music on my computer in my free time for a while now. Because I've lived in several cities in the last 7 years and the equipment required for what I envision would have precluded that, I haven't really invested in putting together a more involved studio. The program used to create these tracks is called Propellerhead Reason and it is a sort of digital emulator for analogue synthesizers. My position on analogue vs. digital electronic music is ostensibly that it's a phony debate, but if I'm being honest, the computer program is a bit limited and I prefer music made with a lot more equipment that exists physically in the world (I'm sort of a nerd like that). As a result, I've basically approached the practice of making computer music as an experimental endeavor meant to learn the theory of using analogue equipment, and in doing so I've made these tracks as sort of vague, unfinished sketches. The longer I do this the better I get at it, the more I like the tracks, and the more I understand how to use the program's parameters to design sound, often with various personally invented methods and in ways that are unique to the program, which stresses me out when I consider what will and will not translate when using other production methods. I do quite like the tracks though so I've included them as sounds on a public website to encourage myself to tweak and shade in certain elements to complete them. More excitingly, I feel ready to settle down and set up a studio, and coincidentally Propellerhead finally included external midi capabilites with the latest version of Reason, so I will be able to fully apply the knowledge I've built with the program's interface to the vast world of electronic musical instruments equipped with the midi protocol.

"Two fixed ideas can no more exist together in the moral world than two bodies can occupy one and the same place in the physical world," writes Aleksandr Pushkin in his short story The Queen of Spades, which inspired this short "score." In the supernatural tale, Hermann, an officer in the Russian Imperial Army, risks it all to learn a mysterious three-card combination passed down to an elderly Countess by the Count of St-Germain in order to win at gambling. Misleading Lizaveta — the Countess's young ward — into believing he is a suitor and thereby gaining entry to her house, he uses the opportunity to accost the elderly Countess and literally scares her to death by demanding the secret at gunpoint. Nevertheless, the Countess's ghost appears to the officer and reveals the cards — three, seven, ace — and advises him to play the hand and marry her young ward. At the gambling table, after winning two consecutive games of Faro in two consecutive nights with the ghostly advice, on the last evening Hermann confidently waits ace in hand for the final card to be revealed. It is ace, and Hermann gleefully announces that he holds the same, but finds — in its place — the Queen of Spades. Lizaveta marries a state official who makes a nice salary, and Hermann, having lost his life savings, is committed to Room 17 at Obukhov Hospital to repeat, "Three, seven, ace! Three, seven, queen!" for the rest of his life. The moral of the story is: play your cards right.

The above image is a picture of Dewey Johnson from the book avant-garde jazz musicians: performing "out there." Having listened to John Coltrane's album Ascension for the first time fully in 2014 and experiencing an eponymously related sensation, I visited Wikipedia to learn more, where Johnson is listed as the only horn soloist on the album who didn't go on to enjoy greater fame. The entry goes on to claim the trumpeter, plagued by mental health issues, never appeared on a professional recording again, disappearing in New York City in the 1980's and ending up at Bird S. Coler Hospital on Roosevelt Island. Given the illustrious subsequent careers of his fellow soloists on the album, the only feasible explanation to me at the time was that Johnson had improvised some sort of magical sequence of notes, which by playing had rendered him in a permanently ascended state, unable to ever play music or even interact on the material plane again. With that impression in mind, I set about imagining what that might be like, and experimented with making a number of short sequences that were eventually adapted into this longer track. Further research into more primary resources unearthed the aforementioned book, and a 1983 album by Paul Murphy entitled Cloudburst, with Dewey Johnson on trumpet duties. It's pretty "out there."

Interview with Ulrich Müller (founder of Architektur Galerie Berlin) by Marta Jecu
Translated from German by Biko Knox for the book Open Monument


Marta Jecu: The idea of the model is still strong in architectural practice and in exhibiting architecture. How do you see its transformation in the last decades and how do you use this polymorphous formula of representing space (including ideal prototype, working maquette, instrument of presentation, and scenographic and illusionistic setting) in your exhibitions? We can take as a starting point, a consideration of the model in a large sense, not necessarily understood as object, miniature, but as a form of distilling the principle that structures space. The present exhibition in your gallery, by the Mexican architect Tatiana Bilbao, is also a condensed representation of a real context, and can be considered a space-filling model. The border between model and installation is blurred here. Is this extended model a medium of representation that presents architecture as art?


Ulrich Müller: This is a very complex question and of course you are right: The architectural model still plays a very important role in the communication of architecture. In spite of all the fantastic new technologies for visualizing architecture only the model allows us to experience the three dimensionality of architecture in the most immediate way. But I want to talk about the model as a contemplative object which has a lot to do with what I'm doing as a curator of the Architektur Galerie Berlin. When I make an exhibition, the model then assumes all possible forms. Insofar it is an aid, but it is not the only means. I see the model as one of the classic ways to show architecture in an exhibition, beside plans, photographs, sketches, documentation, etc. But my idea when I am showing an architecture exhibition is not only to simply document architecture in this — let's say — classic way. My idea is always to create an atmosphere which brings the visitors — either physically or intellectually — as close as possible to the real building or architecture we discuss — in spite of that this is of course absent. That's why I always ask the architects that exhibit in my gallery to visualize their idea of architecture and not only to make a kind of documentation, as is the common practice. At the end, the aim is always to see the gallery space as a huge model which the architects transform with their spatial inventions. In this type of oversized model the architect can represent not only what we see in a real building, but also what cannot be represented in the project itself: one can represent the idea behind it through the logic of the model.


The installation by Tatiana Bilbao — an original wood construction from a Mexican site that divides the gallery space into two levels — has a lot of different meanings, it is a brilliant example of what I have in mind of what an architectural model can be nowadays. Firstly, the wooden construction gives us an exact idea of the construction technology employed in the part of the world where Tatiana is working. Through its seemingly anarchic structure, which is totally different from the high technology we use, the influence of the building process on the finished building becomes transparent. Second, the gallery space is totally changed: it is divided into two levels so that we have a totally different spatial experience now. Thirdly, the main point for an architect is to always present his real work. In the case of Tatiana's show, she totally dispenses with the presentation of the completed buildings. Tatiana designed the exhibition so as to transport an idea and stresses how important the aspect of the construction of the building site is. In this way, her work comes close to arts, since it works on a high level of abstraction.


M. J.: There is an ambiguity between the projective and documentary character of models (and of architecture exhibitions as well): they are both documenting an essential form of buildings, and projecting and anticipating the potential of the discipline itself, surpassing concrete possibilities. How would you consider the role of the model in your curatorial practice from this perspective?


U.M.: Of course, the classic architecture model still plays an important role, as before. Maybe not like it was 15 or 20 years ago, as today the question is more often posed how I visualize an architectural idea. There is a transition from a concrete model-nature to a more abstract one in the presentation of architecture. Many architects ask themselves whether it is possible to exhibit architecture at all. Many exhibition strategies recall a classic architectural model. There is also this intense exchange: artists watch what makes the architecture and architects look at what makes the art. I cannot actually exhibit architecture and in this situation I need to find an alternative solution. It is, just as in art as well, about finding a substitute for reality, where in architecture it has to do with above all something usable. Art reflects reality and interprets the reflection. Many architects look to apply these special opportunities that art offers in architecture. The exhibition looks then as different as possible from the real building. There are also, of course, places in Berlin and in Germany that exhibit architecture in a way that I call classical. In the more than 50 exhibitions that I have done in this gallery up to now (where I do six exhibitions per year), the common architecture model nevertheless still plays an important role in about one of every three exhibitions. The point is to realize that classic presentation media can also be somewhat exhausting and the concern is to find new presentation models of architecture in space. This is why the current exhibition of Tatiana Bilbao is an outstanding example of how architecture is represented as a model of extreme abstraction.


M.J.: In which way do you consider the various representation media and documentation forms of architecture (architectonic photography, drawing or architectural sketch, models) to be constructing the discipline? Can architecture be reinvented and influenced in its concrete realization through its representation?


U.M.: I hope that this is indeed possible. But of course an exhibition is quite an extraordinary format in the complex cosmos of architecture. Most architects build houses rather than engage themselves with theoretical problems. An architecture exhibition, in any case, is but a field of experimentation for which an architect must/can have time, desire, and capacity. I always request the architects to consider the exhibition space as a model workshop, and I ask them to please make something here that they do not otherwise have the chance to do! This is since I believe that creating something in a gallery that only stays for six weeks gives a special freedom that they should use. In the best case this temporary work has an influence on daily architectural work, but I would not overestimate it. I am curious what Jürgen Mayer H. would say about it. He works a lot with exhibitions and ultimately an exhibition serves in essence to reflect and possibly find a faster rhythm.


M.J.: An architecture exhibition also has a virtual character. It represents a concrete realization of virtual architecture. How do you work with this dimension in the exhibitions that you curate?


U.M.: Why does one make architectural exhibitions anyway? The architectural process is protracted and complicated. But usually a good and successful architect does not want to communicate his or her architecture only through a real building standing on a fixed location. A good architect also wants to use other means of communication, to be a part of current debates and enrich them with new arguments. Unlike other forms of communication (images, internet, books etc.) an architecture exhibition provides the work three-dimensionally. And this is essential because architecture lives from the third dimension. In an exhibition, an architect has intermediate formats to develop architectural discourse and bring his own ideas to the public faster and easier. Also he can find a new or different audience with whom to stage a discussion about his architecture. In the temporary format of an exhibition, an architect can realize aspects that he cannot realize in architecture or does not dare to do so, and in turn these can then open new ways for the everyday, in which they can be then applied.


M.J.: A model is also architecture without the social and historical framework for which architecture is meant (an exhibition is theoretically abstracting the social context of a building as well). In recent projects though, the social context becomes more present and almost replaces the focus on the building as an end product.


U.M.: That's right and as we pointed out before: architecture is actually unexhibitable, because it functions only in reality and on a scale of 1:1. Architecture is not only a piece of art, its success also depends on its social components, on the manner in which it is adopted by the user. This important function of architecture you can try to show but not really experience in an exhibition. But as you correctly said, a change takes place in discussing architecture. I'll give you another example: until only about ten years ago, the representation of architecture (especially in photos) worked mostly without any representation of the people that use it. This is gradually changing more and more now: photographers try to show the urban and social context in order to describe how the building works, beside its aesthetic aspects. Ten years ago, it was about a perfectly clean condition of architectural space, which was stripped being photographed using central perspective without people, so that the space appeared visually larger. Architecture has been presented in this sense primarily as an aesthetic phenomenon. Although this has changed in the medium of photography, it is not that easy to transform in this sense architecture exhibitions as well, there are other conditions at play there. Nevertheless, I see a new challenge there for which new forms have to be developed. Maybe we come to the conclusion that an exhibition is after all limited by this point of view and we will finally focus on architecture as the art to build – which is something we should not forget before all the new debates.


M.J.: How did you treat the social context, the lived and transformative dimension of architecture in your last shows?


U.M.: There was, for example, an exhibition of Holzer Kobler Architects, who placed their entire office in the gallery through a kind of photo collage, where it became unclear whether it was a theater stage or a real space. The boundary between exhibition space and the utilized location was blurred. I think it was a brilliant way to connect the social context with aesthetic questions and I hope to generate other shows with this focus in the future.


Biography of Fritz Teufel
Translated from German by Biko Knox for the book Open Monument


Fritz Teufel (June 17th, 1943 - July 6th, 2010, Berlin) became known to the public at large in 1967 as a West Berlin communard, political activist, author, and active participant in the student movement.


Teufel was born during the war, as the last of six children in Ingelheim, Germany in 1943. The family moved to Ludwigsburg in 1946. He came to West Berlin in 1963 and began studying German studies, mass communications, and theater arts at the Freie Universität Berlin. Alongside Dieter Kunzelmann and Rainer Langhans, he was one of the founders of Kommune I, which attracted global attention mainly through its deliberately provocative actions directed against the prevailing social conditions of the day.


Teufel and others were arrested in early 1967, when they were observed throwing "bombs" at then U.S. Vice President Hubert H. Humphrey. The police and press designated this as an assassination attempt on the Vice President, but the projectiles turned out to be bags filled with pudding and flour, and the protest later was dubbed "Pudding Assassination." The day after Humphrey's visit, the alleged bombers were released.


On June 2, 1967, Fritz Teufel was arrested and accused of throwing a stone during a demonstration against Shah Reza Pahlavi of Iran and remained in custody until the start of his hearing in November. From the perspective of the public prosecutor's office, throughout the proceedings Teufel distinguished himself primarily through his utter lack of respect. Wanting to make a long statement, the judge warned Teufel that he could only present facts that served to establish the truth. Somewhat later, he met the judge's request to rise with the remark "If it serves to establish the truth (German: Wenn's der Wahrheitsfindung dient)." The phrase has since become a epea pterontea in the German language. On December 22nd, 1967, Teufel was acquitted.


Over time, Fritz Teufel moved increasingly towards urban guerillism and the path of armed struggle. The manufacture of incendiary devices that had been found in a Munich court earned him two years in prison.


In 1975, as a leading member of the 2 June Movement (German: Bewegung 2. Juni) involved in the kidnapping of Berlin CDU Chairman Peter Lorenz, Teufel was arrested and charged again. After five years in custody, the court proceedings took place in 1980. Only after pleas by the defense and the prosecution had been made — with the latter having asked for fifteen years imprisonment — did Teufel submit an alibi with which he could prove that he had been working at the Pagette factory in Essen under a false name at the time of the crime. The justification for the late presentation of his alibi was that he could "show how a person accused of committing indeterminate offenses was prejudged, and how the whole system worked." He also assumed that he would have been sentenced to five years in prison in connection with illegal weapons possession during his arrest, and in connection with supporting the 2 June Movement. He was in fact released from custody immediately, but the court issued a new indictment for bank robberies in Berlin in which the robber had distributed chocolate kisses to the staff and customers of the banks. For this, Teufel boasted the "B-Libi" that he had been in hiding in Cologne at the time of the crime, but did not wish to name any potential witnesses so as not to subject them to the risk of prosecution as well.


According to a Spiegel interview from November 3rd, 1980, Teufel coined and propagated the concept of "Spaβgerilja" (English: fun guerrilla.): "Spaβgerilja to me is the current form of class struggle," he explained, "Since I have been trying to bring the term Spaβgerilja into circulation, neologisms are my hobby." On February 19th, 1982 he stirred up a storm on during a broadcast on television channel 3 nach 9, where he discussed, among other things, good conduct with then Federal Minister of Finance Hans Matthöfer (SPD). While in conversation with the television presenter, he pulled out a water pistol and squirted the Minister with invisible ink. Matthöfer responded by pouring his glass of wine onto Teufel.


After the conclusion of the trials, Teufel worked as a freelancer for the daily newspaper Taz, and as a bike messenger in Berlin. Suffering from Parkinson's Disease, he had to give up this work. Most recently, he lived in seclusion with his life partner Helene Lollo and friends in Berlin-Wedding. He was awarded The Wolfgang-Neuss Prize for Civil Courage in 2001. In his acknowledgments when receiving the prize Teufel noted, "Thanks to my unborn, unbegotten children, who allow me a life of luxury and joy."


Teufel died of Parkinson's Disease on July 6th, 2010.



From the Wikipedia entry "Fritz Teufel," accessed November 1st, 2013.


Biography of Giovanni Gastel
Translated from French and edited with some art historical context written and added by Biko Knox (paragraphs one through three)


When Giovanni Gastel first encountered photography in the 1970's, the medium was considered a legitimate art form and discipline and had begun to be collected in the world's museums and galleries alike. At the time, fashion photography — which was created and found in a number of contexts but had hitherto been considered a practical, advertorial, and commercial endeavor — was also beginning to gain status and recognition in the art world after work by artists such as Man Ray, Guy Bourdin, Irvin Penn, and Erwin Blumenfeld introduced their surreal visions to publications en masse and thus redefined the photographic canon.


Born in Milan in 1955 to Giuseppe Gastel and Ida Visconti di Modrone, Gastel's first exposure to the arts came through his uncle and renowned film maker Luchino Visconti. His career had its first turning point when he met his future agent Carla Ghiglieri in 1981 and began to work for famous magazines such as Annabella, Vogue Italia, Mondo Uomo, and Donna. Like Bourdin, Gastel maintained a constant artistic output throughout his career in fashion and in 1997 his arrival came when the Triennale di Milano dedicated a solo exhibition to him curated by the contemporary art historian Germano Celant.


Like his predecessors, the artist's work is rife with visual allusions to surrealism but the artist's central concern is the poetic: "What fascinates me about photography is that it has no relation to reality," says Gastel, "It is the interruption of the constant flow of time. From eternal movement, life, to eternal immobility. It alludes to the real to create a real parallel." Like a poet, the artist is primarily interested in realizing a concise structure of form within an image that will be briefly experienced, but imbue meaning to the viewer in its reflective irreality.


In Italy his reputation is on par with major international fashion photographers such as Annie Leibovitz, Mario Testino, and Mario Sorrenti. Monica Belluci, Naomi Campbell, and Linda Evangelista have posed for the artist's perceptive and unique eye. He works regularly for Glamour (Italia), Vanity Fair (Italia), ELLE Decor (Italia), and is one of the officially appointed staff photographers for Harper's International Magazine (US).


Palazzo della Ragione Fotografia/Milan presented another major retrospective of his works curated by Germano Celant entitled Quarana Anni di Fotografia in 2016. His exhibition Donna was held concurrently at Photo12 Galerie in 2016. His latest exhibtion Canons of Beauty was held at the Lumiere Brothers Centre for Photography in Moscow in 2017.


Charlotte Posenenske — Earlier Works
Written by Biko Knox


The things I make are variable, as simple as possible, reproducible. They are components of a space, since they are like building elements, they can always be rearranged into new combinations or positions, thus, they alter the space. I leave this alteration to the consumer who thereby again and anew participates in the creation. The simplicity of basic geometric forms is beautiful and suited to the demonstration of the principles of rationalized modifications.


-Charlotte Posenenske, Statement [Manifesto]


Born in 1930 and active in the late 1950s and 60s, Charlotte Posenenske worked as an early German painter and minimal artist, often exhibiting alongside close friend and artist Peter Roehr before leaving art to pursue sociology in 1968. Posthumously exhibited at documenta 12 in 2007, interest in her work was recently revived in a very big way during the last decade, placing her among the leaders of German minimalism.


Before the conception of her serial, variable modular sculptures, the artist worked as a painter. Heavily influenced by Cézanne, she began with impressionistic painting before moving on to abstract gestural painting, stripe pictures, and structural sculptural pictures, reminiscent of Piet Mondrian in their colors and conception of spacing, or Lucio Fontana in their irregular distribution of marks on the canvas and elusion of specific formation. Her Spritzbilder series experimented with the mechanic reproducibility of the spray paint medium and was an example of the artist striving further to turn away from subject matter without focusing on individual expression to the degree of her Abstract and Informel contemporaries.


Strongly interested in constructivism, the artist often experimented with space as a means of rejecting the idea of art as autonomous practice. Exploring different techniques of applying paint with her striped pictures using felt markers, adhesive strips, colored pencils, chalk, and spatulas, Posenenske realized a series of works with varied brushstrokes in differing orientations and relationships to one another. Like a diagram of spatial possibilities, these works' striking similarity to her later modular reliefs provide a window into her engagement with space before sculpture and a clear link to those sculptures' democratically manipulable modularity, reproducibility, and seriality.


Posenenske described her sculptural pictures as such:


They are reminiscent of impressions from our technical environment: Illumination effects, fast driving, spaces of roads or air that narrow, or bulge forward or bend backwards. At the same time, they are reminiscent of our technological environment — suggesting parts of automotive chassis, billboards, warning signs whose production is similar in terms of technology.


Shifting away from two-dimensional work after she developed her sculptural pictures into a series of relief sculptures — objects with standard geometrical shapes and colors and meant to be serially produced in unlimited qualities and performatively manipulated as those present see fit — Posenenske eventually left the art world altogether. Born to a Jewish family on October 28, 1930, as a child she only escaped the concentration camp during the war through the generosity of friends who helped smuggle her out of Germany and whose courage left her with a lifelong interest in social and political involvement. Working as a sociologist until her death in 1985, Posenenske explained her break with art in her 1968 manifesto Statement, appearing in Art International no. 5:


Art is a commodity of transient contemporary significance, yet, the market is minute, and [as] prestige and prices rise the less topical the supply is. It is difficult for me to come to terms with the fact that art can contribute nothing to solving urgent social problems.


Biography of William Schwartz
Written by Biko Knox


William S. Schwartz (February 23, 1896 — February 10, 1977) was born to a poor family in Smorgon, Russia, where he attended art school on scholarship as a teenager before immigrating to the United States at the age of 17. There Schwartz, who had "chosen his life's work early on," managed to support himself by painting houses and as an opera, vaudeville, radio, and concert singer in order to pursue his ultimate dream as a painter.


First moving to New York to live with his sister and then Omaha to study with J. Laurie Wallace at the Kellom School, Schwartz ultimately ended up in Chicago, where he studied under Ivan Trutnev and Karl A. Buehr at the Art Institute of Chicago. He graduated with honors in life drawing, portraiture, and painting before his career took off in 1926, when he had his first of three solo exhibitions at the Institute in 1926.


The artist — sporting long hair, a thick accent, and a handlebar mustache — became a well known character and was a fixture at Riccardo's Restaurant and Gallery alongside other well-known artists such as Ivan Albright, Malvin Albright, and Aaron Bohrod. In 1921, Schwartz met and eventually married Mona Turner — then a married woman and mother of two — who he adored and benefited from greatly as she became both his muse and "least lenient critic." Together they toured the US by car to visit friends as Schwartz painted the country en plein air.


Throughout the Great Depression he supported himself by painting murals in a regionalist style at numerous post offices for the Federal Art Project. He incited minor controversy with his nude lithographs, and his subject matter moved freely between genres to suit his vision. "I have painted in faith and in freedom," wrote the artist, "faith that somehow what I have done will reflect the best that is in me — freedom to choose my own themes in my own way." Ultimately Schwartz was most known for and worked most prolifically in symbolist and abstract styles, but he characterized the American scene as, "more colorful and challenging than I or any artist might hope to record in a hundred lifetimes." Identifying himself as a "romantic modernist," he was heavily influenced by surrealism and incorporated influences from cubism and constructivism into his abstract works.


In the 1920s he began his series Symphonic Forms, regarded by many as the height of his oeuvre. Merging his two passions — art and music — the paintings from the series feature surreal biomorphic forms and bright colors recalling the fauvist palette. Punctuated by jagged cubist shapes and tonalist hues, his symphonic scenes evoke the timbre, notes, and compositional scale of the vibrant and evolving musical works they were inspired by, such as Beethoven's Symphony No. 23. Recalling Kandinsky's lyrical mysticism, Schwartz's work seems to capture the spirit of music in its variegated form. Writing for the Chicago Tribune in 1970, Schwartz put his painting into his own words:


"My subjects are the beauty and wonder, the laughter and poetry, the pity and terror of the present world, which is the only universe I know. My drawing, composition, color, and techniques are based on the best education I could come by and extended to the limits of my own creative invention and capacities. I do not imitate. Therefore I, and I alone, am responsible for the vision of the world I set before the viewer."


Moving between different styles and influences, inspired by the American landscape and subject matter, Schwartz's surreal visual opuses stand out as stunning reminders of his work as a uniquely abstract modernist.





CV


My professional experience includes roles at William Siegal Gallery in Santa Fe, NM (ancient South American art and textiles), the Studio of Marina Abramović in New York (performance art), Galerie Mehdi Chouakri in Berlin, Germany (contemporary conceptual art), William Karges Fine Art in Los Angeles (American impressionistic, post-impressionistic, modern, and abstract painting), and Photo12 Galerie in Los Angeles (contemporary photography), where I assist owner in managing locations internationally in Paris, Los Angeles, and Shanghai.


I am experienced in art writing, press relations, digital presence management, German-to-English translation, French-to-English translation, art installation, art photography, image editing and archival, managing art fair participation, and sales. Having double-majored in Journalism at NYU's Arthur L. Carter Journalism Institute and in Political Science at the Wilf Family Department of Politics, which emphasizes both a statistically-based quantitative approach and formal political analysis, I enjoy writing, editing, and the dissemination of information covering a diverse (and preferably erudite) range of topics, and analyzing and understanding social trends on a large and small scale.


I have found that art provides many alternate modes of exploration in relation to perception, thought, viewing history, and conceiving the future and have thoroughly enjoyed the various roles I've held supporting its communication, production, and exhibition. I am also personally passionate about digital and analogue electronic music composition. Most recently I expanded my interest in the digital domain to include this website, where I plan to share more music, writing, and other media in the future.


More to come...
